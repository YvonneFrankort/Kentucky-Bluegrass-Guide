About Kentucky

Kentucky, also known as the Bluegrass State, is located in the south-central region of the United States of America. Bordered by seven states, it is a land of deep rivers, rolling hills, and fertile valleys.
Kentucky’s history is shaped by early pioneers, the American frontier, and the rise of agriculture and industry.
Today, the state is famous for its horse racing and farms, bourbon distilleries, bluegrass music, Appalachian heritage, southern hospitality and scenic landscapes that stretch from the Appalachian Mountains in the east to the Mississippi River in the west.
From the bustling streets of Kentucky's largest city Louisville to the scenic countryside of Lexington and the historic state capital of Frankfort, Kentucky blends natural beauty with a rich cultural tradition.


About Bluegrass Region

Located in central Kentucky, the Bluegrass Region is often considered the heart of the state. The region was named for the long-stemmed grass that flourishes there. Its gently rolling hills, fertile limestone soil, rich tobacco fields and pastures give the land its signature deep green shade. This natural environment has made the region the “Horse Capital of the World”, home to world-famous thoroughbred farms and racetracks in Lexington.
The region also holds a deep cultural and musical heritage — it’s the birthplace of bluegrass and country music, gospel.
Historic towns such as Lexington and Frankfort preserve the charm of early Kentucky while offering modern life filled with art, festivals and Southern cooking.

Kentucky Landscapes

Kentucky's landscape is diverse, featuring the forests of the Appalachian mountains in the east, the rolling hills of the Bluegrass Region and the Pennyroyal Plateau with its caves and sinkholes.
The state’s natural beauty includes the sandstone cliffs of the Red River Gorge and Natural Bridge, the expansive Mammoth Cave system, the powerful waterfalls at Cumberland Falls and the peaks of the Appalachians.

National Parks & Nature

Kentucky offers a rich variety of state and national parks that draw hikers, campers, and nature lovers. Mammoth Cave National Park features the world’s longest known cave system, while Red River Gorge offers dramatic cliffs and rock formations popular with climbers. Other scenic destinations include Cumberland Falls — known as the “Niagara of the South” — and Land Between the Lakes, a vast recreation area perfect for boating, wildlife watching, and stargazing.
Bluegrass Region is home to various parks such as Natural Bridge known for its 65-foot tall sandstone arch, one of Kentucky’s most iconic rock formations. Red River Gorge, part of the Daniel Boone National Forest, is famous for its sandstone cliffs, natural arches and forest trails. Kentucky Horse Park, just outside of Lexington, is a working horse farm and educational park focusing on Kentucky’s horse-breeding heritage.

Cultural Heritage

Kentucky is known for horse racing, bourbon and bluegrass music, all part of its unique culture. The Kentucky Derby in Louisville celebrates not just horse racing but also fashion, food and festivity.
The Bluegrass Area is one of Kentucky’s richest cultural areas — blending music, agriculture, craftsmanship, and Southern tradition.
Bourbon-making has been part of Kentucky life for over 200 years and Bluegrass Region lies along the Kentucky Bourbon Trail.
Horse breeding and racing define the Bluegrass Region. Keeneland Race Course in Lexington is famous for its horse races and Louisville as host for the Kentucky Derby.

Cities & Attractions

Lexington
The city is known as the "Horse Capital of the World" and the cultural center of the inner Bluegrass Area.
Frankfort
Frankfort is Kentucky's State Capital. Located along the Kentucky River it combines historic government buildings with scenic hills.
Louisville
Louisville is Kentucky's largest city, located at the Ohio River and at the edge of the Outer Bluegrass. The city is home to the world-famous Kentucky Derby.
